Myrtle Beach Beaches

Piers

Piers are a big part of the Myrtle Beach experience because they give you views over the water, places to fish, and an easy way to enjoy the ocean without needing a boat. Three well-known options are Second Avenue Pier, Cherry Grove Pier, and Springmaid Pier. Prices and hours can change depending on the season, so it is always smart to check before you go.

Second Avenue Pier is on the Myrtle Beach Boardwalk. According to the pier's FAQ, general admission is $2, it was built in 1936, and it is 906 feet long. Their FAQ also says a daily fishing pass is $10 and you do not need to buy a separate fishing license to fish from the pier.

Cherry Grove Pier is in North Myrtle Beach. Their "Pricing and Hours" page lists a $3 walking pass, and a one-day fishing pass with your own rod as $3 admission plus $10 per rod.

Springmaid Pier is known for being one of the longest and most accommodating piers in South Carolina. It is described as 1,060 feet long and 36 feet wide.

Hub of the Grand Strand

Myrtle Beach sits in the middle of the Grand Strand, which is a long stretch of connected beach communities. It is described as the local piers being spread out along 60 miles of shoreline, from the northern end near Cherry Grove down toward Garden City. Because the coastline is connected, you can stay in Myrtle Beach and still explore nearby beach towns that feel slightly different from one another.

Atlantic Ocean

Myrtle Beach faces the Atlantic Ocean, which is why the entire area is built around beach days, ocean views, and pier activities. Ocean conditions change daily, so it is smart to pay attention to beach flags, weather, and posted safety signs, especially during busy summer months. One summer there were jellyfish hoarding around the piers, so there was an advisory to stay out of the water near them. We didn't know and my little cousin got stung by one. They weren't super venomous or deadly, but it sure left a mark on him.
